Abstract

Marston Alfred Hamlin was an artist, educator, and an active participant in the 20th century American labor movement. The Marston Alfred Hamlin Papers (dated 1937-1963) document his professional, political, and personal life. This collection primarily covers Hamlin's his experiences with the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC)and his work as a political cartoonist focused on labor organizing. Hamlin's career as an artist is documented through original artwork, pamphlets, sketchbooks, exhibition flyers, notebooks, notes, and correspondence. His employment history and labor organizing are documented through written testimony from his family, personal correspondence, and publications and ephemera related to his employers, including Sperry Gyroscope, the United Nations, Washington University, and Ohio State University. His personal life and experiences with the House Un-American Activities Committee are documented in correspondence with family and friends, notebooks, court transcripts, and written testimonies.

Scope and Contents

The Marston Alfred Hamlin Papers (dated 1937-1963) consist of materials created and collected by Hamlin documenting his professional, political, and personal life. His work as an artist and labor organizer is documented in original artwork, exhibition flyers, correspondence, sketchbooks, notes, newspaper clippings on exhibitions, pamphlets containing his illustrations, and documentation of his involvement in labor organizing. Correspondence, notes, and notebooks also document Hamlin's career as an arts professor at Washington University in St. Louis and Ohio State University, where his employment was terminated after he refused to answer questions before the Ohio House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC). His relations with the House Un-American Activities Committee are documented in correspondence, newspaper clippings, and collected court transcripts.
