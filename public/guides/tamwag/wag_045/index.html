Historical Note

The Newspaper Guild was founded December 15, 1933; journalist Heywood Broun was its first president. In New York, the Guild's first contract was negotiated with the New York Post in 1935. The Guild, which includes locals in Canada and Puerto Rico, as well as throughout the United States, called itself the American Newspaper Guild (ANG) until 1971, when it changed its name to the present The Newspaper Guild (TNG). From the mid-1970's to the mid-1980's (the period covered by the Papers, with the exception of some material from 1967) Local 3 membership stood at roughly 5,000 and the International at about 30,000.

Wade was nominated by petition both times she ran for the presidency of Local 3 (as-opposed to the union's more usual procedure in which a candidate is nominated by the local's Representative Assembly by a quorum of member representing a certain number of shops). Her election to her second term (which began in 1980) was challenged by the losing candidates, who forced a re-run of the election (supervised by the Department of Labor) in September 1982. Wade did not run for president in this election re-run; instead she ran for membership on the Local's Executive Committee. She and all the members of her insurgent Membership Slate were defeated in this election. In December 1982 Wade also ran unsuccessfully for the office of Local Executive Vice President.

Wade also served three times as a regional president of the Guild's International. She ran unopposed for her first term (as was usual in elections for this position), but the elections for her next two terms were contested.

Scope and Content

It is important to note first what these Papers are not. They are not the official files of the Newspaper Guild nor of Local 3 (the Papers' organizational records of Local 3 and TNG are fragmentary and incomplete), nor are they the personal papers of Betsy Wade, although they incorporate elements of both. They are, instead, Betsy Wade's personal collection of records of and relating to the Newspaper Guild and its Local 3-mostly in the periods from 1975 to 1984-and materials given to Wade by Rose Mary Mechem that relate to the Guild's policies regarding involvement in (United States) government-supported or government-sponsored organizations and programs in Latin American and elsewhere abroad.

The focus of most of these documents is on the activities of Wade and an "insurgent caucus" with which she was associated in the International and in Local 3. These materials document a lively struggle between the insurgents and their supporters (variously called the Reponsibility Slate, the Membership Slate, etc.) and the insurgents' opponents (different individuals and slates variously called the Democratic Coalition, the Representative Assembly Slate, the Unity Slate, etc.) at every level of the organization and on practically every issue.

Throughout the collection-which covers a fairly broad spectrum of Guild concerns and activities, and in which are represented all levels of the organization-this conflict is evident. But nowhere is it better or more clearly illustrated than in the group of materials concerned with elections and referenda (Series II, subseries 10, "Elections, Referenda, Disputes"). Here are gathered materials on more than twenty-five elections, referenda and public disputes that took place in Local 3 in the years from 1978 to 1984. These materials (largely, though not entirely, campaign literature), which amount to over one-third of the collection form the heart of the Papers, for it is here that the most concentrated, chronological picture of the issues and personalities at the core of the conflicts may be seen.

Another group of materials in the Papers, however, concentrates on one particular aspect of these conflicts: disagreements at the Guild-wide level over Guild involvement in government-supported or government-sponsored programs in foreign countries. Series IV (Foreign Affairs) documents the struggle, within the Guild over this issue, which began as early as 1967, with revelations of CIA involvement in the American Institute for Free Labor Development (AIFLD) that resulted in the termination of TNG-AIFLD joint programs, and re-emerged in the late 1970's and early 1980's with the resumption of these programs. Much of this material is drawn from documents gathered by Rose Mary Mechem and given to Betsy Wade, although a significant amount also comes from Wade, who also collected materials on the subject.

While, as had been said, the focus of the Papers is on the internal struggle in the Guild, the collection is also a good source of information on a broader range of concerns that have to do with the administration and activities of a labor union. the minutes of the International Executive Board of TNG, of the Executive Committee of Local 3, and the Representative Assembly and General Membership Meeting of Local 3 are particularly useful in this regard. While these by no means represent complete runs of all the years indicated, some are close to complete.

Also of particular value, and interfiled throughout the collection, are handwritten notes. Some of these are merely marginal notations written directly onto various documents (such as meeting agendas) that record, for example, whether Wade attended a particular meeting or when and how many copies of a leaflet were distributed. Others, however, are far more extensive: personal minutes, for example, of meetings, and notes on telephone conversations, with some dialog recorded verbatim. On occasion these notes are separate individual documents, but more often they are written on the front or back of preexisting documents such as leaflets. For this reason the researcher should take care to examine both sides of all documents in the Papers. Most of these notes appear to be in Wade's hand. A sample of Wade's handwriting is included in the appendices, to assist the researcher in making this determination.
