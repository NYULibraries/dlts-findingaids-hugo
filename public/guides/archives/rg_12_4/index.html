Abstract

The Office of the Registrar is responsible for the integrity of academic policies, the student information system and student records from application to degree conferral. The office works to protect and preserve the academic records of New York University's students and to provide a framework for students to navigate the academic process. The Records of the Office of the Registrar consist of 18 handwritten ledgers dating between 1832 and 1916. The majority of the ledgers track applications for admission, student matriculation, class rolls, grades, and citations for meritorious conduct.

Scope and Contents

The Records of the Office of the Registrar comprise handwritten ledgers dating between 1832 and 1916. The majority of the ledgers track applications for admission, student matriculation, class rolls, grades, and citations for meritorious conduct. A scrapbook compiled by the college's first registrar, Leslie J. Tompkins, documents the construction of and repairs to several buildings on the Heights Campus; endowments, scholarships, and prizes granted to students; commencement and lecture programs; and annual reports from the Treasurer's and the Registrar's offices. One volume lists officers and platoons, possibly in an early campus security organization; roll calls and lists of officers sent on patrol; and entries documenting arrests. One volume consists of a letterbook, containing letters written by J. J. Stevenson as registrar and Pomeroy Ladue as secretary regarding admission, coursework, and commencement in the graduate school in the 1890s.
